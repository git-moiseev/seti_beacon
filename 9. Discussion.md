# 9. Discussion

The waveform presented in this paper should be regarded as one possible realization of a more general design philosophy rather than as a unique solution.

The central objective is to minimize the computational effort required for blind detection by reducing receiver uncertainty in successive stages.

The proposed implementation achieves this objective through continuous transmission, constant transmitted power, narrow occupied bandwidth, deterministic frequency geometry, and deterministic temporal structure.

None of these individual design choices is new.

Continuous carriers, frequency-shift keying, packet repetition, and complementary coding are all well-established techniques.

The novelty of the proposed approach lies in their organization.

Rather than optimizing individual waveform components independently, the proposed design treats them as successive sources of observable information.

Each newly observable property is introduced only after the receiver has completed the previous stage of discovery.

This sequential disclosure reduces the dimensionality of the remaining estimation problem while preserving a simple transmitted waveform.

An interesting theoretical question concerns the minimum amount of structural information required for blind discovery.

The proposed waveform employs exactly two frequency states.

A single frequency state provides no internal frequency reference, while additional frequency states increase waveform complexity and occupied bandwidth.

This suggests that two frequency states may represent the simplest spectral geometry capable of supporting Doppler estimation through internal signal structure.

The present work does not attempt to prove this conjecture.

Instead, it proposes a waveform that appears to satisfy this principle while maintaining continuous transmission, constant power, and narrow occupied bandwidth.

Whether this minimality can be established formally remains an open question for future research.

More generally, the proposed waveform should be viewed as an example rather than a final design.

The proposed waveform should therefore be regarded as a reference architecture rather than a definitive solution. Its primary purpose is to demonstrate that waveform structure can be deliberately organized to reduce the computational effort required for blind detection.

Developing a formal optimization framework for such waveforms represents an important direction for future work.

Likewise, the present work deliberately avoids assumptions regarding a particular application domain.

The proposed design principles are applicable to any communication system in which the receiver possesses only limited a priori knowledge of the transmitted waveform and must perform blind detection before synchronization and decoding.

Future work should therefore focus on formal performance analysis, receiver algorithms, optimal parameter selection, and the mathematical characterization of minimum structural information required for computationally efficient blind detection.
