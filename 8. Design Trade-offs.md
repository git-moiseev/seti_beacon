# 8. Design Trade-offs

The proposed waveform minimizes the computational cost of blind detection by introducing only a small amount of additional signal structure.

This simplification is not obtained without cost.

The waveform design is governed by several competing constraints.

## 8.1 Frequency-State Separation

The separation between the two frequency states, $2e$, determines both the required spectral resolution and the observation time needed to resolve the waveform.

Reducing $e$ decreases the occupied bandwidth and makes the waveform approach the ideal single-carrier signal.

However, smaller frequency separation requires longer observation time before the two frequency states can be distinguished.

Conversely, increasing $e$ simplifies frequency-state discovery but increases the occupied bandwidth.

The parameter $e$ therefore represents a compromise between spectral efficiency and observation time.

---

## 8.2 Observation Time and Frequency Stability

Increasing the observation time improves spectral resolution and detection sensitivity.

In practice, however, coherent observation time is limited by

- Doppler drift,
- oscillator instability,
- propagation effects,
- and receiver frequency accuracy.

Consequently, the frequency-state separation cannot be made arbitrarily small.

The optimum value depends on the maximum coherent integration time achievable by the receiver.

---

## 8.3 Packet Duration

Long packet duration improves spectral purity and increases the efficiency of coherent integration.

However, longer packets delay the observation of the complementary packet repetition required for temporal synchronization.

Short packets reduce synchronization latency but increase spectral broadening due to more frequent frequency transitions.

Packet duration therefore represents a trade-off between spectral concentration and synchronization time.

---

## 8.4 Why Constant Power Requires Two Frequency States

Maintaining constant transmitted power while conveying binary information imposes a fundamental constraint on the waveform.

If only a single carrier frequency is available, binary transmission requires periodically switching that carrier on and off. During one binary state the transmitter radiates energy, while during the other it remains silent.

Consequently,

- the transmitted power is no longer constant,
- the signal spends approximately half of its time absent from the channel,
- the receiver loses integration time,
- the waveform acquires an unnecessary amplitude structure.

These characteristics directly contradict the design objective of continuous transmission with constant emitted power.

The smallest modification that preserves both information transfer and continuous transmission is the introduction of a second frequency state.

Instead of switching between transmission and silence, the transmitter continuously radiates while alternating between

$$
F_0-e
$$

and

$$
F_0+e.
$$

Binary information is therefore represented by frequency selection rather than transmitter activity.

At every instant the transmitter radiates the same total power, satisfying the constant-power requirement while preserving continuous observability.

---

## 8.5 Why Two Frequency States Are Sufficient

Once two frequency states are available, the receiver can determine every parameter required for blind acquisition:

- carrier midpoint,
- frequency separation,
- Doppler shift,
- waveform consistency.

Introducing additional frequency states increases signal complexity without providing fundamentally new information.

A third frequency state does not improve Doppler estimation, since the midpoint is already uniquely defined by two frequencies.

Likewise, additional states do not increase robustness against narrowband interference, because any interference affecting one frequency can already be rejected by verifying the expected two-frequency geometry.

More frequency states also increase

- occupied bandwidth,
- transmitter complexity,
- search space,
- receiver processing cost,

while providing no corresponding reduction in acquisition uncertainty.

Therefore, two frequency states represent the minimum complete frequency geometry: sufficient to resolve the ambiguity inherent in a single carrier, yet no more complex than necessary for blind acquisition.

Under the design objective of minimizing receiver computational cost, increasing the number of frequency states beyond two yields diminishing returns without introducing additional information required by the acquisition process.

## 8.6 General Remarks

The objective of the proposed waveform is not to maximize any individual performance metric.

Instead, the waveform balances bandwidth, observation time, synchronization latency, and computational complexity in order to minimize the overall effort required for blind detection.

The resulting design should therefore be viewed as an engineering compromise rather than an optimum with respect to a single criterion.
