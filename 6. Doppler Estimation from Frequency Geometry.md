# 6. Doppler Estimation from Frequency Geometry

A continuous single-frequency transmission minimizes occupied bandwidth and simplifies initial detection. However, once the signal has been discovered, the receiver still cannot distinguish the nominal carrier frequency from the unknown Doppler shift.

The proposed waveform resolves this ambiguity by introducing two known frequency states symmetrically positioned around the nominal carrier frequency,

$$
F_0-e
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
F_0+e.
$$

After propagation through a channel producing a Doppler frequency offset $\Delta f_D$, the receiver observes

$$
f_-=F_0+\Delta f_D-e,
$$

and

$$
f_+=F_0+\Delta f_D+e.
$$

The midpoint of the two observed frequencies immediately provides an estimate of the Doppler-shifted carrier,

$$
f_c=\frac{f_-+f_+}{2},
$$

from which the Doppler offset is obtained,

$$
\Delta f_D=f_c-F_0.
$$

The known separation between the two frequency states,

$$
f_+-f_-=2e,
$$

provides an internal consistency check for the received waveform.

Rather than searching for an arbitrary spectral pattern, the receiver searches for a simple and deterministic frequency geometry.

Once this geometry has been identified, the Doppler uncertainty is eliminated from all subsequent stages of processing.

The two-state waveform therefore serves two independent purposes.

First, it preserves the desirable properties of a continuous narrowband transmission during blind detection.

Second, it introduces the minimum additional spectral structure required to estimate the Doppler offset without increasing the dimensionality of the initial search problem.

This illustrates the central design principle proposed in this work: every newly observable signal property should simplify the remaining estimation problem.
