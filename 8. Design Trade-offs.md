# 8. Design Trade-offs

The proposed waveform minimizes the computational cost of blind detection by introducing only a small amount of additional signal structure.

This simplification is not obtained without cost.

The waveform design is governed by several competing constraints.

## 8.1 Frequency-State Separation

The separation between the two frequency states, $2e$, determines both the required spectral resolution and the observation time needed to resolve the waveform.

Reducing $e$ decreases the occupied bandwidth and makes the waveform approach the ideal single-carrier signal.

However, smaller frequency separation requires longer observation time before the two frequency states can be distinguished.

Conversely, increasing $e$ simplifies frequency-state discovery but increases the occupied bandwidth.

The parameter $e$ therefore represents a compromise between spectral efficiency and observation time.

---

## 8.2 Observation Time and Frequency Stability

Increasing the observation time improves spectral resolution and detection sensitivity.

In practice, however, coherent observation time is limited by

- Doppler drift,
- oscillator instability,
- propagation effects,
- and receiver frequency accuracy.

Consequently, the frequency-state separation cannot be made arbitrarily small.

The optimum value depends on the maximum coherent integration time achievable by the receiver.

---

## 8.3 Packet Duration

Long packet duration improves spectral purity and increases the efficiency of coherent integration.

However, longer packets delay the observation of the complementary packet repetition required for temporal synchronization.

Short packets reduce synchronization latency but increase spectral broadening due to more frequent frequency transitions.

Packet duration therefore represents a trade-off between spectral concentration and synchronization time.

---

## 8.4 Number of Frequency States

The proposed waveform employs exactly two frequency states.

A single frequency state minimizes bandwidth but provides no information for Doppler estimation.

Introducing two states creates the minimum frequency geometry required to estimate the Doppler offset.

Additional frequency states may provide further information, but they also increase waveform complexity, occupied bandwidth, and the number of hypotheses that must be evaluated during blind detection.

For this reason, two frequency states represent the simplest geometry capable of supporting hierarchical signal discovery.

---

## 8.5 Why Two Frequency States?

The proposed waveform employs exactly two frequency states.

This choice is motivated not only by engineering simplicity but also by the amount of structural information required by the receiver.

A single continuous carrier is sufficient for estimating the Doppler offset when the nominal transmitter frequency $F_0$ is known:

$$
\Delta f_D=f_c-F_0.
$$

However, a single frequency state provides no internal spectral structure that can be independently verified by the receiver. It also cannot represent two binary states through frequency selection while preserving continuous constant-power transmission.

Introducing a second frequency state therefore does not make Doppler estimation possible; rather, it adds a deterministic spectral relationship that supports waveform validation, continuous binary signaling, and limited resistance to narrowband interference.

The receiver may estimate the center frequency,

$$
f_c=\frac{f_-+f_+}{2},
$$

while simultaneously verifying the expected frequency separation,

$$
f_+-f_-=2e.
$$

These two observations provide both Doppler estimation and an internal consistency check without introducing unnecessary waveform complexity.

Additional frequency states could convey further structural information or support additional objectives, but they are not required for binary transmission, Doppler estimation, or continuous constant-power operation.

Also, they would also increase occupied bandwidth, waveform complexity, and the number of possible receiver hypotheses during blind detection.

From this perspective, two frequency states appear to constitute the simplest waveform capable of providing an internal frequency reference.

Whether this minimality can be established formally remains an open theoretical question.

## 8.6 General Remarks

The objective of the proposed waveform is not to maximize any individual performance metric.

Instead, the waveform balances bandwidth, observation time, synchronization latency, and computational complexity in order to minimize the overall effort required for blind detection.

The resulting design should therefore be viewed as an engineering compromise rather than an optimum with respect to a single criterion.
