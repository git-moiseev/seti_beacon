# 7. Temporal Structure Discovery

Once the carrier frequency and Doppler offset have been estimated, the remaining uncertainty is primarily temporal.

The receiver still does not know

- the packet duration,
- packet boundaries,
- symbol timing,
- or whether the transmission is periodic.

The waveform should therefore provide deterministic temporal structure while introducing as little additional complexity as possible.

A simple solution is to transmit each information packet twice, with every second transmission replaced by its bitwise complement,

$$
P,\;
\overline{P},\;
P,\;
\overline{P},\;
\ldots
$$

where

$$
\overline{P}=
(1-b_1,\,
1-b_2,\,
\ldots,\,
1-b_N).
$$

This transmission protocol provides several useful properties simultaneously.

First, every information bit is transmitted once in each frequency state over every complete packet pair.

Consequently, the transmitted energy is equally distributed between the two frequency states regardless of the packet contents.

Second, the temporal waveform acquires a deterministic periodic structure.

After reconstructing the binary frequency-state sequence

$$
q(t)\in\{-1,+1\},
$$

the receiver observes

$$
q(t+T_P)=-q(t),
$$

and

$$
q(t+2T_P)=q(t),
$$

where $T_P$ is the packet duration.

The packet duration therefore becomes observable without requiring a dedicated synchronization sequence.

More importantly, the receiver is not required to search for arbitrary packet boundaries.

Instead, it searches for a deterministic temporal relationship already implied by the waveform design.

Temporal synchronization thus becomes another stage of hierarchical signal discovery rather than an independent blind estimation problem.
