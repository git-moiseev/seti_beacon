# 5. Hierarchical Disclosure of Information

The proposed waveform is designed so that different signal properties become observable only after the receiver has completed the previous stage of processing.

Rather than solving one large multidimensional estimation problem, the receiver performs a sequence of progressively simpler tasks. Each stage reduces the uncertainty presented to the next stage, thereby decreasing the overall computational cost of blind detection.

## 5.1 Stage 1 — Energy Detection

Initially, the receiver possesses only two pieces of information:

- the nominal carrier frequency $F_0$,
- the maximum expected Doppler offset $d$.

No assumptions are made regarding

- waveform structure,
- symbol timing,
- packet duration,
- modulation,
- coding,
- synchronization.

The receiver therefore performs only an energy search within the frequency interval

$$
F_0-(d+e)
\le f \le
F_0+(d+e).
$$

At this stage, the waveform appears simply as a continuous narrowband signal.

No further structure is required.

---

## 5.2 Stage 2 — Frequency-State Discovery

Once the signal has been detected, the receiver may increase the observation time to obtain higher spectral resolution.

The receiver now discovers that the transmitted waveform does not occupy an arbitrary frequency, but only one of two possible frequency states.

The known separation between these states,

$$
2e,
$$

provides the first deterministic property of the waveform.

Instead of searching an arbitrary spectrum, the receiver now searches for a known spectral geometry.

This substantially reduces the probability of false detection.

---

## 5.3 Stage 3 — Doppler Estimation

After both frequency states have been observed, the receiver estimates their center frequency,

$$
f_c=\frac{f_-+f_+}{2}.
$$

The Doppler offset is then obtained as

$$
\Delta f_D=f_c-F_0.
$$

The frequency search is now complete.

Subsequent processing no longer requires searching over Doppler uncertainty.

---

## 5.4 Stage 4 — Temporal Structure Discovery

After frequency uncertainty has been eliminated, the receiver begins to investigate the temporal behavior of the waveform.

The transmitter repeatedly emits the information packet followed by its bitwise complement,

$$
P,\;
\overline P,\;
P,\;
\overline P,\;
\ldots
$$

This creates a deterministic relationship between consecutive packet repetitions,

$$
q(t+T_P)=-q(t),
$$

and

$$
q(t+2T_P)=q(t).
$$

The receiver may now estimate

- packet duration,
- packet boundaries,
- symbol timing.

No frequency search is required during this stage.

---

## 5.5 Stage 5 — Information Recovery

Only after all physical parameters of the waveform have been determined does the receiver attempt to recover the transmitted information.

At this point the original blind detection problem has been transformed into a conventional communication problem with known carrier frequency, known Doppler offset, known packet timing, and known waveform structure.

The computationally expensive multidimensional search has therefore been replaced by a sequence of substantially simpler estimation problems.
