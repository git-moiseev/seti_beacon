# 4. A Hierarchical Waveform

The simplest waveform satisfying the design principles established in the previous section is a continuous narrowband carrier transmitted at the nominal frequency $F_0$.

Such a signal possesses several desirable properties.

It occupies the minimum possible bandwidth, radiates continuously at constant power, and allows arbitrarily long coherent integration. From the perspective of blind detection, these characteristics make the waveform nearly ideal.

However, after detection the receiver gains almost no additional information.

The measured carrier frequency immediately reveals the combined effects of the nominal transmitter frequency and the unknown Doppler shift,

$$
f_c = F_0 + \Delta f_D,
$$

but these quantities cannot be separated.

Consequently, the receiver has detected the signal but has learned nothing about the Doppler offset required for subsequent synchronization.

This observation suggests that the waveform should expose additional information only after detection has already been completed.

To achieve this objective, the transmitted signal is allowed to occupy one of two fixed frequency states,

$$
F_0-e
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
F_0+e,
$$

where

$$
0 < e < d.
$$

At every instant, the transmitter occupies exactly one of these two frequency states while radiating its full available power.

The transmitted waveform can therefore be expressed as

$$
s(t)=A\cos\left(2\pi\left[F_0+e\,q(t)\right]t+\phi(t)\right),
$$

where

$$
q(t)\in\{-1,+1\}.
$$

The function $q(t)$ determines the instantaneous frequency state of the transmitter.

Unlike conventional digital communication systems, these two frequency states are introduced primarily to simplify blind detection rather than to increase communication efficiency.

The waveform preserves all desirable properties of the single-carrier solution:

- continuous transmission,
- constant transmitted power,
- deterministic behavior,
- extremely narrow occupied bandwidth.

At the same time, it introduces a simple geometric relationship that becomes available only after the receiver has improved its spectral resolution.

The transmitted waveform is therefore no longer optimized solely for detection, nor solely for information transfer.

Instead, it is optimized to reveal progressively more information as the receiver advances through successive stages of observation.
