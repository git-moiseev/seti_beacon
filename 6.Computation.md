# 6. Computational Perspective

The proposed waveform does not reduce the amount of information that must ultimately be recovered by the receiver.

Instead, it reduces the amount of uncertainty that must be processed at each stage of blind discovery.

This distinction is fundamental.

In a conventional blind detector, multiple unknown signal parameters must often be estimated simultaneously. Frequency, timing, modulation, coding, and synchronization become components of a single multidimensional estimation problem.

The computational complexity of such a detector generally increases with both the dimensionality of the hypothesis space and the resolution required along each dimension.

The proposed waveform follows a different strategy.

Each processing stage estimates only those parameters that are observable at the current level of uncertainty.

The result is a sequence of progressively simpler estimation problems,

$$
\mathcal{H}_0
\supset
\mathcal{H}_1
\supset
\mathcal{H}_2
\supset
\cdots
\supset
\mathcal{H}_N,
$$

where $\mathcal{H}_i$ denotes the remaining hypothesis space after completion of stage $i$.

The receiver never attempts to estimate parameters that cannot yet be observed.

Instead, every completed stage removes one or more degrees of freedom from the remaining search problem.

The waveform is therefore designed to maximize the reduction of hypothesis space produced by each stage of observation.

This principle may be expressed informally as

> Every newly observable signal property should eliminate more uncertainty than it introduces.

The objective is not to minimize signal complexity.

The objective is to maximize the reduction of receiver uncertainty produced by each increment of observable signal structure.

Consequently, waveform design becomes an optimization problem over the sequence of observable properties rather than over the transmitted signal alone.

The proposed two-state waveform represents one possible realization of this principle, but the principle itself is independent of the particular modulation or signaling method.
